Herrenberg Ruling: Adjunct Lecturers at Universities | Alphabees

The Herrenberg ruling could force adjunct lecturers at universities into mandatory social insurance. What consequences loom and how AI-powered learning support can help.

The so-called Herrenberg ruling by the Federal Social Court from 2022 is currently causing considerable unrest in German higher education. Originally concerning freelance teachers at music schools, the ruling could soon be applied to adjunct lecturers at universities as well. The consequences would be far-reaching: many adjunct lecturers previously considered self-employed would have to be hired as employees subject to social insurance. For education managers and university leadership, the urgent question arises of how to respond to this development – and what role digital solutions like AI-powered learning support could play.

The Crucial Role of Adjunct Lecturers in Higher Education

Adjunct lecturers form a central pillar of German university teaching. The figures from the Federal Statistical Office illustrate the scale: with around 95,000 individuals, their number exceeds that of professors by approximately 82 percent. The ratio is particularly pronounced at universities of applied sciences – here, more than two adjunct lecturers work for every professorship. At public administration colleges, the ratio reaches as high as 4.5 to 1.

These figures underscore how heavily the higher education system depends on external expertise. Adjunct lecturers bring practical professional experience from companies, clinics, cultural institutions, and other organisations into academic teaching. They ensure that degree programmes remain connected to professional reality and that students benefit from current practical insights.

Until now, these professionals have typically been employed part-time on a fee basis. They sign a freelance contract with the university, invoice their hours, and pay taxes on the income as self-employed individuals. This flexible arrangement allows universities to integrate specialised competencies on short notice and as needed.

What the Herrenberg Ruling Means for Universities

The Herrenberg ruling fundamentally challenges this established practice. In 2022, the Federal Social Court ruled that freelance teachers can be considered dependent employees depending on how their work is actually structured – regardless of what the contract states. The decisive factors are whether the teacher is integrated into the organisation's structure and bears no genuine entrepreneurial risk.

Should this ruling be applied to universities, it would have significant financial consequences. Personnel costs would increase by approximately a quarter due to social security contributions – without creating any additional teaching capacity. With hourly rates currently ranging between 24 and 80 euros, this surcharge quickly adds up to a substantial amount.

Additionally, administrative burden would increase significantly. Each individual case would need to be reviewed, and processing employment relationships subject to social insurance requires additional administrative resources. Music academies, where active artists provide a large proportion of teaching, face particularly existential challenges.

The Transition Period and the Pressure to Act

Currently, a transition period is in effect that grants universities a reprieve. According to the current status, this deadline is to be extended until the end of 2027. After that, case-by-case assessments would again determine whether adjunct lecturers are classified as self-employed or dependent employees.

The CHE Centre for Higher Education Development urges swift action. Simply extending the transition period does not solve the problem but merely postpones it. A legislative clarification that takes into account the particularities of university teaching would be more sensible. Conceivable options include thresholds for the scope and duration of teaching activities, below which adjunct lecturers could continue to work as self-employed.

For education managers, this situation means two things: on one hand, they must closely monitor legal developments and adapt their contract models if necessary. On the other hand, they should already be considering strategies for maintaining teaching capacity even with rising costs or departing adjunct lecturers.

This relief is particularly valuable when personnel resources become scarcer or adjunct lecturers leave due to new legal requirements.

Conclusion: Act with Foresight Now

The Herrenberg ruling marks a potential turning point for the organisation of university teaching in Germany. Whether and how the ruling will be applied to adjunct lecturers remains to be seen. What is certain, however, is that universities and educational institutions are well advised to prepare early for various scenarios.

The combination of political engagement for legal clarifications and the strategic use of digital learning support appears to be a promising path forward.
