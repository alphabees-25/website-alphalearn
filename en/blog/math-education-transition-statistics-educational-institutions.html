Math Education: Key Statistics & Opportunities for HE

Current data on mathematics education reveals declining advanced courses and a growing need for teachers. Discover the opportunities this presents for educational institutions.

International Mathematics Day on March 14th draws attention to the importance of this core subject. Recent data from the Baden-Württemberg State Statistical Office provides revealing insights into the development of mathematics education at schools and universities. For decision-makers in educational institutions, this yields important findings – and concrete areas for action.

Advanced Mathematics Courses: Declining Trend Despite High Relevance

In the 2024/25 school year, approximately 26,300 students in Baden-Württemberg enrolled in advanced mathematics. This represents 43 percent of all upper secondary students at general education schools. At first glance, this rate appears stable – but looking at the trend over recent years reveals a different picture.

Since the 2020/21 school year, participation in advanced mathematics courses at general education grammar schools has declined by roughly ten percentage points. This decline raises questions: Is mathematics losing its appeal? Or do students lack the foundational skills needed to choose this demanding subject?

Also noteworthy is the difference between school types: While 43 percent of upper secondary students at grammar schools choose advanced mathematics, only 34 percent do so at comprehensive schools. This discrepancy points to varying prerequisites and support opportunities that students receive on their mathematical education journey.

Mathematics Degrees: Teacher Training Boom Amid Declining Overall Interest

At Baden-Württemberg universities, just over 8,100 students were enrolled in mathematics programs in the winter semester 2024/25. This represents approximately two percent of all students. Within this group, a remarkable trend emerges: Around 64 percent of mathematics students are pursuing a teaching qualification.

Particularly striking is the high proportion of women in mathematics teacher training: 74 percent of prospective mathematics teachers are female. In other mathematics degree programs, however, male students dominate at 66 percent. This distribution has long-term implications for the composition of teaching staff in schools and thus for role models available to students.

The long-term view reveals a trend: While mathematics teacher training has grown by 19 percent over the past ten years – driven primarily by a 26 percent increase in female students – other mathematics degree programs have shrunk by 21 percent. This decline affects both women and men.

Areas for Action for Universities and Training Providers

The figures highlight several challenges that education leaders should address:

  • Strengthen early support: The decline in advanced courses signals that mathematical competencies need to be developed more systematically before upper secondary level.
  • Enable individual support: Not all learners have equal access to qualified tutoring or support. Digital learning companions can close this gap.
  • Optimally prepare teacher training students: Given the growing demand for mathematics teachers, universities must ensure that students are best prepared for their profession.
  • Increase the appeal of STEM subjects: The decline in non-teaching mathematics degree programs requires new approaches to inspire students toward these fields.

In all these areas, intelligent learning technologies can make a decisive contribution. They enable personalized learning paths, provide support exactly when needed, and simultaneously relieve educators of repetitive support tasks.

Conclusion: Mathematics Education Needs New Impulses

Current statistics on mathematics education are a wake-up call for education leaders. The decline in advanced courses, the disparity between school types, and falling student numbers in non-teaching degree programs demonstrate: Action is needed.

At the same time, the growing field of teacher training presents an opportunity. Those who optimally train prospective teachers and equip them with modern tools are investing in the mathematical education of future generations.

For decision-makers at universities, academies, and training institutions, the question arises: How can we make mathematics education more effective – without overloading our capacities? AI-powered learning support offers a forward-looking answer.
