Dual Training in Decline: AI in Professional Development | Alphabees

New apprenticeship contracts in Germany's most populous state fall below 100,000 for the first time. Education leaders must consider how modern technologies can enhance the attractiveness and quality of vocational training.

Recent figures from North Rhine-Westphalia mark a historic turning point: for the first time since records began in the 1970s, fewer than 100,000 new apprenticeship contracts were signed in the dual system. With 99,246 new contracts in 2025, a trend continues that equally concerns education leaders and decision-makers in companies, chambers, and training institutions. The question of how vocational education can regain its appeal has never been more pressing.

Understanding the Scale of the Decline

The decline in apprenticeship numbers is not a short-term phenomenon. Over a ten-year comparison, new contracts dropped by a significant 14.4 percent. The industry and commerce sector is particularly affected, recording the sharpest decline at 21.4 percent compared to 2015. The skilled trades, traditionally a stable pillar of dual training, also experienced a 4.8 percent decrease.

For decision-makers in education and training, these figures represent more than abstract statistics. They concretely mean:

  • Intensified competition for qualified young talent
  • Rising demands on the quality and attractiveness of training offerings
  • The necessity to reduce training dropouts and strengthen persistence motivation
  • Pressure to deliver high-quality support despite limited resources

At the same time, comparison with university enrollment figures reveals a notable development: in 2024, nearly equal numbers of people began university studies as started dual training programs. Academic education has clearly gained ground in young people's perceptions – a trend that vocational training must counter with modern concepts.

Structural Causes Require Structural Solutions

The reasons for the decline are multifaceted. Demographic factors play a role, as do changing educational preferences. However, attributing the shift solely to external factors would be short-sighted. The quality of the training experience itself is under scrutiny.

Today's apprentices expect a learning environment that matches their digital lifestyle. They are accustomed to accessing information at any time and receiving quick answers to their questions. Traditional training structures, where knowledge is primarily conveyed through personal guidance in the workplace, reach their limits here. Not because personal supervision is unimportant – quite the opposite. Rather, because trainers' time resources are limited and not every question can be answered immediately.

This opens new opportunities for education leaders through the targeted use of technology. AI-powered learning support can bridge precisely those gaps that arise in daily training routines without replacing the human element.

Strategic Perspective for Education Leaders

Declining apprenticeship numbers are a wake-up call. They signal that business as usual is insufficient to maintain dual training as an attractive educational pathway. For decision-makers at universities, academies, chambers of commerce, and companies with their own training programs, the question of future-proof concepts arises.

Integrating AI-powered learning support is not an end in itself but a response to concrete challenges. It enables organizations to offer high-quality support with limited personnel resources that meets the expectations of digitally native learners. At the same time, it strengthens vocational education's position in competition with academic offerings.

The figures from North Rhine-Westphalia clearly demonstrate the need for action. The industry and commerce sector, with a 7.5 percent decline compared to the previous year, has particularly lost attractiveness. Yet trends are also negative in skilled trades and liberal professions. Those who want to strengthen vocational education must invest in quality – and modern technology is an essential lever for this.

Dual vocational training remains a success model that enjoys international recognition. For it to continue attracting enough young people in the future, it must evolve. AI-powered learning support offers the opportunity to combine traditional strengths with contemporary assistance, setting the course for future-proof vocational education.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is the number of apprenticeship contracts declining in Germany?
Demographic shifts, declining school leaver numbers, and the growing appeal of academic pathways result in fewer applicants for dual training programs. At the same time, many training companies lack the capacity for intensive individual support.
How can AI-powered learning support improve training quality?
AI tutors provide apprentices with round-the-clock individual assistance for technical questions, enabling continuous support. This relieves trainers while simultaneously improving the learning experience for apprentices.
What role does digitalization play in making dual training more attractive?
Modern digital learning tools can make training more contemporary and flexible. For digitally savvy young people, this increases attractiveness compared to purely traditional training formats.
Is AI-powered learning support suitable for small craft businesses?
Yes, smaller businesses particularly benefit from scalable solutions that integrate into existing learning platforms without major IT investment. This ensures apprentices in small companies also receive high-quality learning support.
How can education leaders measure the success of AI tutors?
Key metrics include exam pass rates, training dropout rates, and apprentice satisfaction. AI systems can also transparently document learning progress and flag support needs early on.
